# Symptom Development

> Source: Kerr & Bowen, *Family Evaluation* (1988), Chapters 7 and 9

## Overview

Family systems theory links all clinical symptoms — physical, emotional, and social — to the emotional system. Symptoms are not random breakdowns in an individual; they are products of a disturbance in the balance of the emotional system, both within a person and within his relationship network. The theory bridges the conventional compartmentalization of disorders into "medical," "psychiatric," and "conduct" categories by showing that the same basic patterns of emotional functioning contribute to all of them.

A central premise: **the patterns of emotional functioning that contribute to the development of physical illness are the same as those that contribute to the development of emotional or social illness.** This does not mean emotional process *causes* disease. It means the emotional process in a family is a major influence on an individual's ability to adapt successfully to the presence of factors that can precipitate illness.

## The Relationship Between Anxiety and Symptoms

### Anxiety as the Engine

Symptoms are rooted in the undifferentiated or togetherness aspects of human functioning. When members of a relationship system function in reaction to one another — based on automatic emotional reactivity rather than thoughtful autonomy — someone in the system is likely to get into a position that impairs functioning.

**The feedback loop:**
1. Anxiety rises in the system (from internal or external sources)
2. Emotional reactivity intensifies among family members
3. Patterns of emotional functioning (conflict, spouse dysfunction, child focus) become more active
4. The intensified patterns generate more anxiety
5. Self-control erodes; people become automatic reactors to the emotional environment
6. Clinical symptoms emerge

> Anxiety → emotional reactivity → pattern intensification → more anxiety → more reactivity → **symptoms**

See [Chronic Anxiety](chronic-anxiety.md) for the distinction between acute and chronic anxiety.

### Acute vs. Chronic Symptoms

- **Acute symptoms** are associated with short-term disturbances in system balance. A sudden stressor (death, job loss, medical crisis) temporarily overwhelms the system's adaptive capacity.
- **Chronic symptoms** are associated with long-term disturbances. The patterns of emotional functioning have been rigidly active for extended periods, often years or decades.

### The Togetherness Paradox

Symptoms can be both *generated* and *relieved* by the togetherness process:

| Togetherness generates symptoms when... | Togetherness relieves symptoms when... |
|---|---|
| People pressure one another to think, feel, and act in specific ways | A symptomatic person develops a comfortable relationship ("feeling at one with the other" is calming) |
| An important relationship that sustained someone's functioning is disrupted (death, divorce) | A symptomatic person gets more emotional distance from overwhelming interactions |

However, symptom relief through togetherness is related to an increase in *functional* level of differentiation — it is dependent on the relationship continuing. An increase in *basic* level of [differentiation](differentiation.md) can reduce symptoms more durably, without that reduction being dependent on any particular relationship.

## Where Symptoms Occur: The Three Categories of Dysfunction

The level of [differentiation](differentiation.md) and the level of [chronic anxiety](chronic-anxiety.md) determine a relationship system's *overall* vulnerability to symptom development. *Where* a symptom occurs — in which person or which relationship — is determined by which pattern of emotional functioning predominates in that family.

### 1. Dysfunction in a Spouse

One spouse absorbs a disproportionate share of the family's anxiety through physical, emotional, or social dysfunction. This is the overfunctioning/underfunctioning reciprocity described in the [Nuclear Family Emotional System](nuclear-family-emotional-system.md).

**Mechanism:**
- One spouse progressively adapts to preserve harmony, yielding self to the relationship
- The adaptive spouse's functioning erodes over time
- The other spouse gains functional self at the adaptive one's expense
- The adaptive spouse becomes increasingly vulnerable to illness

**Clinical examples:**
- A wife who chronically defers to her husband's preferences gradually loses initiative, develops depression, then chronic fatigue
- A husband who "keeps the peace" by suppressing his own needs develops hypertension, then a cardiac event
- A mother who has been chronically psychotic since her second child — during average anxiety, she is reasonably stable with medication and psychiatric support; when family anxiety spikes, the emotional patterns that foster her dysfunction are accentuated and she requires hospitalization

**Key insight:** The symptomatic spouse is not necessarily the person with the most individual pathology but the one who has absorbed more of the relationship's undifferentiation. When one parent's functioning is impaired, the other parent may function *better* in reaction to it.

### 2. Marital Conflict

Family anxiety is externalized into the relationship between husband and wife. Neither spouse gives in; the undifferentiation is expressed through overt disagreement, opposition, and mutual blame.

**Mechanism:**
- Each spouse focuses on what is "wrong" with the other
- Anxiety that might otherwise be absorbed by one person's dysfunction is bound in the conflict
- When parents are in conflict, the binding of anxiety in the marital relationship frees the children to function better

**Clinical examples:**
- A marriage that is mildly conflictual at baseline becomes highly conflictual under stress; children remain relatively unaffected
- Two parents at differentiation level 50 who, when anxious, transform a reasonably harmonious marriage into a volatile one — the conflict absorbs the anxiety and neither parent nor child becomes individually symptomatic

### 3. Impairment of One or More Children

Parental anxiety is focused on a child through the [Family Projection Process](family-projection-process.md). The child absorbs the family's anxiety and develops symptoms.

**Mechanism:**
- Parents become preoccupied with what is "wrong" with one of their children
- The child absorbs parental anxiety and becomes increasingly anxious himself
- The child develops physical, emotional, or social symptoms
- Parental anxiety is bound through the focus on the child, sparing the marital relationship

**Clinical examples:**
- Two parents at differentiation level 25 whose individual functioning and marriage are largely unimpaired, but both children have serious chronic physical health problems; when family anxiety increases, parents intensify their focus on the children's physical well-being, "infecting" the children with more anxiety and exacerbating each child's chronic condition
- A family where intense focus on one particular child leads that child to function at a schizophrenic level while siblings function adequately; had the focus shifted among the children, all might have escaped that level of impairment

### Emotional Distance as a Crosscutting Feature

Emotional distance is not a distinct category of dysfunction because it is a feature of all relationships and intertwined with all patterns. However, it is as important a manifestation of undifferentiation as the others. See [Emotional Cutoff](emotional-cutoff.md) for the extreme form.

## How the Categories Distribute in Families

### The Conservation Principle

The degree to which the undifferentiation of a nuclear family system is absorbed in one relationship or in the poor functioning of one person is the degree to which other relationships and other people are *protected* from dysfunction. Undifferentiation is neither created nor destroyed — it is redistributed:

- If one parent's functioning is impaired → the other parent and children may function better
- If a child's functioning is impaired → siblings and parents may function better
- If parents are in conflict → children are freed to function better

### Differentiation Level and Pattern Distribution

| Differentiation Level | Pattern Activity | Symptom Profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Very low (e.g., 25)** | All three patterns intensely active | Multiple family members chronically symptomatic — both parents, the marriage, and several children may be seriously dysfunctional |
| **Low-moderate** | Two patterns absorb most anxiety | E.g., father has chronic alcoholism AND one child is delinquent; mother and other children have no significant symptoms |
| **Moderate** | One pattern absorbs most anxiety | Anxiety contained within one relationship or one person's poor functioning |
| **Well differentiated** | Patterns present but mild | Average chronic anxiety too low for significant clinical symptoms under normal circumstances |

**Critical point:** Regardless of differentiation level, *any* nuclear family can develop symptoms under sufficient stress. Even well-differentiated families can be overwhelmed if stress is protracted and exceeds adaptive capacity.

### Pattern Flexibility and Prognosis

The patterns of emotional functioning that predominate in a family *can shift over time:*

- Early in marriage: one spouse adapts more → spouse dysfunction during high anxiety
- Later: the pattern shifts to marital conflict → neither spouse becomes symptomatic, but harmony disappears
- The principal pattern of binding anxiety changes, but the basic relationship problem (the balance of individuality and togetherness) remains the same

**Flexibility is protective.** When the family emotional process shifts around — affecting different members at different times — it is less likely that any one person's functioning will be chronically impaired. Large discrepancies in family members' functioning are most likely when there is *little flexibility* in how the undifferentiation is played out.

**Example of rigidity:** If family anxiety is bound in an intense, unshifting focus on one particular child, that child may function at a schizophrenic level while siblings function adequately. Had the focus been more distributed, all children might have achieved enough emotional separation to avoid severe impairment.

## Origins: Why Families Develop Particular Patterns

The particular category or categories of dysfunction peculiar to a nuclear family are determined largely by the experience each parent had growing up in his or her family of origin. While growing up, everyone learns to adapt to the relationship process in their particular family — and a child plays his part in creating and reinforcing that process.

### The Emotional Mirror Image

When people form new relationships, they tend to select mates with whom they can replicate the more influential aspects of the relationship process from their original family. People gravitate toward their **emotional mirror images** — even when they are determined not to let it happen.

The "fit" or "complementarity" between partners:
- Is part of the comfort of a relationship and may promote cohesion
- Evolves out of what each person learned about managing anxiety in relationships
- Under increased family anxiety, the basic elements of the complementarity become exaggerated
- The nature of these exaggerated elements determines whether the problem emerges as marital conflict, spouse dysfunction, or child dysfunction

### Nature and Learning

Saying that adaptation patterns are "learned" does not mean they are based on strictly psychological or cultural factors. The patterns are assumed to be **anchored in the instinctual nature of man:**

- All the various ways of adapting to anxiety in relationship systems are part of everyone's natural makeup
- The automatic inclination to make certain behavioral adjustments in response to anxiety does not have to be learned
- All animals appear to make similar adjustments in response to perceived threats
- Learning determines *which* patterns are most active in a particular person and family
- This learning occurs on several levels, from conditioning of emotional responses to incorporation of subjectively determined attitudes, values, and beliefs

## A Systems Model of Disease

### Beyond Cause and Effect

A systems model contrasts fundamentally with a cause-and-effect model of disease:

| Cause-and-Effect Model | Systems Model |
|---|---|
| Illness is produced by the overwhelming impact of a pathogenic process | Many factors interact to produce a clinical syndrome |
| Resistance is overcome by the nature of the external assault or internal defect | Each factor affects the others; none can be understood in isolation |
| Disease can be explained at one level of investigation | All levels of investigation are necessary for adequate explanation |
| "What has caused this disease?" | "How has the balance of relationships within and around this person been disturbed?" |

### The Balance Metaphor

People can have cancer cells and tubercular bacilli in their bodies and not get clinical symptoms. Clinical symptoms indicate that a balance has been disturbed. The systems question is not "What is the pathogen?" but "What disturbed the balance that was keeping the pathogen quiescent?"

**The AIDS example:** If a person is not infected with HIV, he cannot develop AIDS. A systems model does not deny that a specific pathogenic agent may be *necessary* for a specific disease. But it asks: how has the harmonious balance of relationships within the "sick" person and within his central relationship network been disturbed? The disturbance in balance permits whatever pathogenic processes are present to become more active.

### Multi-Level Understanding

Albert Scheflen's (1981) research on schizophrenia exemplifies the systems model. He identified processes operating on as many as **eight levels** — from the molecular to the societal — that create and reinforce the disorder:

1. **Molecular/genetic level** — genetic predispositions, neurotransmitter abnormalities
2. **Physiological level** — brain structure and function abnormalities
3. **Psychological level** — disturbed thinking patterns
4. **Family level** — relationship processes that create and sustain schizophrenic functioning
5. **Institutional level** — hospitals that "control psychosis but sustain schizophrenia" by perpetuating dependency
6. **Community level** — social attitudes and support systems
7. **Cultural level** — cultural definitions of illness and normalcy
8. **Societal level** — economic and political structures that affect treatment

**Key insight from Scheflen:** The schizophrenic person's dependency and relationship sensitivity make him prone to withdraw into a psychotic state, and the psychotic state hampers his ability to function independently. Asking whether the psychosis leads to the dependency or the dependency leads to the psychosis is like the chicken-and-egg debate — both are components of a circular system.

### The Iatrogenic Risk

Treating a clinical dysfunction on just one level can reinforce the processes that are ignored:

| Single-Level Treatment | Unintended Systems Consequence |
|---|---|
| Individual psychotherapy for a focused-on child | May relieve symptoms but reinforce the [family projection process](family-projection-process.md) — child is viewed even more as "the one with problems" |
| Medication for a child's chronic asthma | May control symptoms but reinforce the child's position as repository of family anxiety |
| Steroids for lupus + telling the spouse to help the patient "avoid stress" | May encourage the spouse to overfunction even more, undermining the patient's ability to regain control of her emotional and physical functioning — more togetherness to "solve" problems created by togetherness |
| Hospitalizing a psychotic patient | May control psychosis but sustain dependency — a core component of schizophrenic functioning |

### The Alternative: Multi-Level Systems Thinking

An alternative to cause-and-effect models: all levels are assumed to be equally important and mutually reinforcing. Disturbances on one level are not *blamed* on disturbances on another level:

- The "patient" is no more the cause of the family's distress than the family is the cause of the "patient's" distress
- Institutions cannot be blamed for fostering dependence any more than patients can be blamed for creating the need for institutions
- The psychology of schizophrenia cannot be blamed for creating the biology any more than the biology can be blamed for creating the psychology

**A systems model is not a set of "rules" to follow, but a framework for keeping the total picture in mind.** The goal is for the health professional not to reinforce the very processes they are trying to treat.

### LeShan's Cancer Work

Lawrence LeShan (1977) developed a psychotherapeutic approach to cancer patients who had failed all available medical treatments. His results:

- **50% cure rate** among "medically hopeless" patients
- Treatment addressed biological, psychological, and spiritual levels
- He defined the spiritual level as "the deep and basic need to have a meaningful framework of existence"
- The cancer patient is conceptualized as a person who has "lost his way" — with despair about ever being able to "sing his own song"
- Therapeutic goal: not to cure the cancer, but to help the patient "live under his own name"
- Rather than "the problem being bigger than the person," help the person become "bigger than the problem"

LeShan's work demonstrates that therapeutic approaches on levels other than the biological can have profound effects on biological processes — consistent with a systems model that assumes relationship processes within people and between people are interrelated.

## The Multigenerational View of Symptoms

### Symptoms Don't Appear de Novo

A family member whose functioning is unstable in most aspects is not spawned by a nuclear family whose average functioning is stable. Very unstable functioning in one person is usually associated with unstable functioning in other family members in the existing and preceding few generations. See [Multigenerational Transmission Process](multigenerational-transmission.md) for the full mechanism.

### The Downward Spiral

Each generation that emerges with less differentiation than the preceding one tends to have:
- Higher average chronic anxiety
- More active patterns of emotional functioning
- Greater vulnerability to symptom development
- More rigid binding of anxiety in fewer patterns

### The Upward Spiral

Conversely, children who emerge from the family emotional process with more differentiation than their parents tend to:
- Have lower average chronic anxiety
- Develop more flexible patterns of emotional functioning
- Demonstrate greater resilience under stress
- Form nuclear families that are more adaptive

### Quantum Jumps

Marked discrepancies in functioning can occur in as few as three generations — a "quantum jump." For example, a fairly stable nuclear family can have a descendant functioning at a chronic schizophrenic level within three generations. However, it is more common for such marked decreases in functioning to require five to ten generations. Similarly, pronounced increases in functioning over three to four generations are possible but uncommon.

### The Role of Sibling Position

The child who is most focused upon in the family projection process separates emotionally the least from parents and emerges with the lowest differentiation. Other children, less focused upon, may emerge with differentiation equal to or greater than their parents. This creates diverging lines of differentiation within the same family — one line trending down, another trending up — visible across the family diagram. See [Family Projection Process](family-projection-process.md) for selection mechanisms.

## Symptom Categories

Family systems theory does not distinguish between types of symptoms in the way conventional medicine does. All symptoms are linked to the same underlying emotional processes. The form a symptom takes depends on the combination of many factors — genetic predisposition, exposure to pathogens, environmental conditions — but the emotional process determines vulnerability.

### Physical Symptoms

When the predominant pattern fosters dysfunction in one person, that person is more vulnerable to becoming ill in response to:
- Exposure to allergens or toxins
- Genetic predisposition to disease
- Exposure to infectious agents (viruses, bacteria)
- Pre-existing physiological vulnerabilities

Examples: chronic asthma, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, cancer, cardiac disease, autoimmune disorders

### Emotional Symptoms

The same patterns that create vulnerability to physical illness create vulnerability to emotional disturbance:
- Depression, anxiety disorders, panic
- Psychotic episodes, chronic schizophrenic functioning
- Eating disorders
- Obsessive-compulsive patterns

### Social Symptoms

Social dysfunction follows the same emotional process:
- Substance abuse and addiction
- Delinquency, criminal behavior
- Social irresponsibility
- Chronic underachievement
- Relationship instability

**The bridge:** A person's chronic anxiety level and the degree to which the emotional system has compromised their adaptive capacity determine which type of symptom develops, in combination with whatever genetic, environmental, and circumstantial factors are present. The emotional process is the common denominator.

## Modifying Symptoms

### Two Pathways to Symptom Reduction

1. **Increase in functional differentiation** — symptom reduction that is dependent on relationship conditions:
   - Developing a comfortable new relationship
   - Getting more distance from overwhelming interactions
   - Changes in the emotional environment that reduce pressure on the symptomatic person
   - Life-threatening symptoms can be modified this way

2. **Increase in basic differentiation** — symptom reduction that is independent of relationship conditions:
   - Can occur in the symptomatic person or in someone closely connected to them
   - If a closely connected person raises their basic level, they will do less to impair the functioning of the symptomatic one — staying in emotional contact while permitting the symptomatic person to assume responsibility for their own problems
   - If the symptomatic person raises their own basic level, they can be in contact with their emotional environment without being impaired by it
   - Durable change that does not depend on maintaining or disrupting any particular relationship

### The Challenge of Severe Symptoms

When symptoms are debilitating or life-threatening, it can be difficult to find the time and energy required to modify basic level of differentiation. The immediate crisis must often be managed through functional-level changes (including medical treatment) before work on basic-level change is feasible.

## Clinical Applications

### Assessment Questions

Drawing from George Engel's (1977) formulation: **"Why does this patient have this disease now?"** An answer requires evaluating data from many levels:

1. What is the current level of chronic anxiety in the family system?
2. Which pattern(s) of emotional functioning are most active?
3. How is the undifferentiation distributed — concentrated in one person/relationship or spread?
4. What stressors have accumulated recently (even "positive" ones)?
5. What multigenerational trends are visible in the family diagram?
6. What is the degree of emotional cutoff from extended family?
7. What biological, genetic, or environmental factors are present?

### Therapeutic Principles

1. **Keep the total picture in mind.** A systems model is a framework, not a set of rules. Treatment on one level (e.g., medication) is not wrong — but the clinician should be aware of how single-level treatment may reinforce processes on other levels.

2. **Don't reinforce the problem.** The greatest iatrogenic risk is treating the symptom in a way that reinforces the pattern that created it — more togetherness to solve a togetherness problem.

3. **Work on multiple levels.** The most effective approaches address biological, psychological, and relationship processes simultaneously without assuming any one level is primary.

4. **Address the system, not just the symptom bearer.** If one person in a family can increase their basic differentiation, it changes the emotional environment for everyone — often including the symptomatic person.

5. **Track process, not content.** The specific symptoms matter less than the patterns of emotional functioning that create vulnerability to them.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---|---|
| Family emotional process *causes* disease | Emotional process influences vulnerability; specific illness depends on the combination of many factors including genetics, pathogens, and environment |
| Physical illness is fundamentally different from emotional or social illness | All three are linked to the same patterns of emotional functioning in the family system |
| Treating the symptom is sufficient | Single-level treatment may relieve symptoms while reinforcing the processes that created them |
| The symptomatic person is the "sick" one | The symptom bearer has absorbed a disproportionate share of the system's undifferentiation |
| Symptoms appear randomly in families | Which person develops symptoms is determined by the predominant patterns of emotional functioning — which are themselves shaped by multigenerational process |
| A well-differentiated family never has symptoms | Any family can develop symptoms under sufficient stress; well-differentiated families are simply less vulnerable and more resilient |
