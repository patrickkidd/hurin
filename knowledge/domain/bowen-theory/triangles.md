# Triangles

> Source: Kerr & Bowen, *Family Evaluation* (1988), Chapter 6; supplemented by Gilbert (2006)

## Overview

The triangle is the **basic molecule of an emotional system** — the smallest stable relationship unit. A two-person system is inherently unstable; when anxiety rises, a third person (or issue, or substance) is automatically drawn in to stabilize the dyad. What and how and when and where are facts about a relationship that can be observed. Triangles describe the **what, how, when, and where** of relationships, not the why.

Bowen chose the term "triangle" over the existing term "triad" to signal that he was describing something fundamentally different from prior group-dynamics concepts. The triangle concept illustrates the thinking on which the entire family systems theory is based: an attempt to define facts of functioning in human relationships — facts which can be observed to repeat over and over so consistently that they become **knowable and predictable.**

> "Conjecture about why something happens is not fact and so the inclusion of such conjecture in the theoretical concepts was avoided as much as possible."

The assignment of **motive** is necessarily subjective and not verifiable; the assignment of **function** can be objective and potentially verifiable. This distinction is essential to systems thinking.

## The Basic Processes of Triangles

### The Instability of the Two-Person System

A two-person system may be stable as long as it is calm, but since that level of calm is very difficult to maintain, **a two-person system is more accurately characterized as unstable.** Every relationship between two people who have emotional significance to one another is subject to disturbance by emotional forces within it and from outside.

When anxiety increases, a third person becomes involved in the tension of the twosome, creating a triangle. The involvement of a third person decreases anxiety in the twosome by **spreading it through three relationships.**

### Why Triangles Are More Stable Than Dyads

The formation of three interconnected relationships can contain more anxiety than is possible in three separate relationships because:

1. **Pathways are in place** that allow the shifting of anxiety around the system
2. This shifting reduces the possibility of any one relationship emotionally "overheating"
3. The ability to spread and shift tension, as well as to contain more of it, makes a triangle **more flexible and stable** than a two-person system

### Triangles Are Permanent

Triangles are forever, at least in families. Once the emotional circuitry of a triangle is in place, it usually **outlives the people who participate in it.** If one member of a triangle dies, another person usually replaces him.

> "The actors come and go, but the play lives on through the generations."

Children may act out a conflict that was never resolved between their great-grandparents. A particular triangle was not necessarily created by its present participants; nor does it form anew or completely dissolve with the ebb and flow of anxiety.

Rather than dissolve, triangles become **more or less active** with fluctuations in the level of anxiety:
- In unusually quiet periods, a triangle may be so inactive that its basic relationship processes are not observable
- In unusually chaotic periods, so many triangles are intensely active that it can be difficult to perceive any order in the process
- **Triangles are most easily observed during periods of moderate tension**

## Triangle Dynamics at Different Anxiety Levels

### Low Anxiety: The Insider-Outsider Configuration

When anxiety in the emotional field of a triangle is low:
- Two people are comfortably close — the **insiders**
- The third is a less comfortable **outsider**

This is not a static system; even during calm periods it is in constant motion:
- Both insiders continually make adjustments to preserve their comfortable togetherness, lest one become uncomfortable and form a togetherness with the outsider
- The outsider is not idly standing by, but continually attempts to form a togetherness with one of the insiders
- All participants make **predictable moves** to achieve their ends

**Clinical Example — The Family Dance:**

A husband, when on the outside (in fact or fantasy) of the relationship between his wife and oldest daughter, becomes sullen. The wife predictably reacts to his sullenness by focusing more on him and attempting to cheer him up. The daughter, in reaction to being on the outside between her two parents, becomes overly solicitous toward her father. The mother, reacting to being on the outside between husband and daughter, criticizes the daughter's physical appearance. The daughter responds defensively, and she and her mother engage in a long discussion to resolve their differences. **The system is never still.**

In calm periods: **insiders are trying to preserve what they have; the outsider is trying to break into it.**

### Moderate Anxiety: Active Recruitment and Shifting

As anxiety increases, the comfortableness of the insiders' relationship is eroded. The dynamics shift:

1. The discomfort is typically felt far more by one person than the other
2. The one who is still comfortable may be largely oblivious to the other's unhappiness (a product of how both manage tension)
3. The uncomfortable one first attempts to restore comfortable equilibrium with the other insider
4. Due to higher anxiety, people are more reactive, making harmony more difficult
5. When unsuccessful, the uncomfortable one **initiates a togetherness with the outsider**
6. The outsider, eager for this, responds quickly to the invitation

In a calm emotional field, the insiders work to exclude an outsider. **In a moderate anxiety field, the outsider is actively recruited for more involvement.**

#### Mechanisms of Triangling a Third Person

There are several ways a twosome can incorporate a third person into its tension:

1. **Complaints and side-taking:** The uncomfortable insider (A) pulls the outsider (C) into the situation through complaints about the other insider (B). If C responds sympathetically, taking sides with A, a comfortable closeness (based on undifferentiation) is established between A and C. B becomes the new outsider. **The key element is side-taking.** A and C blame B for the problems in the A-B relationship.

2. **Overflow:** A twosome involves a third person simply by allowing them to be within earshot. The problem "overflows" onto them.

3. **Active self-insertion:** A third person plays a very active role in getting himself in the middle. Through years of "training," such a person has learned to gravitate to disharmony he senses in others, regardless of whether an "invitation" is actually extended. A poorly differentiated child often occupies this type of position with his parents — **predictably making himself a problem whenever tension reaches a certain level between his parents,** drawing one or both parents' focus to him, thus reducing the tension between them.

#### The Moderate-Tension Pattern

At moderate tension levels, the outcome of the shifting process is:
- **One uncomfortable or conflictual relationship** and **two fairly comfortable ones**
- The discomfort or tension may shift from relationship to relationship
- But at moderate tension levels it can usually be **contained within one relationship**

**Clinical Example — Father-Daughter Conflict:**

As long as an oldest daughter and her father are in conflict, harmony can be preserved between the parents and between the daughter and her mother:

- The mother adapts (on some issues) to the father to keep peace → harmony in the marriage
- The daughter sympathizes with her mother's attitude that her husband treats her like a "doormat" → harmony in the mother-daughter relationship
- The marital tension does not disappear — it is **acted out as conflict between father and daughter**

There are still two positive sides in the triangle and one negative side. **A calm two-person relationship may actually be a calm side of a triangle — the calmness maintained at the expense of a negative relationship in another side.**

### High Anxiety: The Outside Position Becomes Desirable

During high stress periods, the emotional process assumes new characteristics. Now **the outside position is the most comfortable and desired.** Each member of an overly intense two-person relationship works to get an outside position to escape the tensions.

**Clinical Example — Mother-Father-Son:**

A mother, caught in intense conflict with her son, actively recruits the father to "deal" with the son. When the father attempts to do so, conflict erupts between father and son and mother withdraws. The son counters by trying to precipitate conflict between his parents — pleading with mother to get the "harsh" father off his back. When anxiety subsides, mother and son again get close and father is excluded.

**Clinical Example — Husband-Wife-Mother:**

A husband who, wanting to avoid a fight that would disturb the togetherness with his mother, triggers conflict between his wife and mother by complaining to his wife about his mother. The relationship between wife and mother is then regarded as "the problem."

This is **not necessarily a diabolical scheme** — it is often done without much awareness of what is happening. When tension is reduced, the wife may again be pushed to the outside of the mother-son relationship. She then makes predictable moves to gain an inside position: *"You care more about your mother than you care about me!"*

## Stabilization and Destabilization: Four Principles

The impact of adding or removing a third person to a twosome can be summarized:

| Scenario | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Stable twosome destabilized by adding a third** | The third person's presence drains time and energy from the dyad | A harmonious marriage becomes conflictual after the birth of a child |
| **Stable twosome destabilized by removing a third** | The third person who was being triangled is no longer available to absorb tension | Marital disharmony increases after a child leaves home |
| **Unstable twosome stabilized by adding a third** | The twosome shifts its anxious focus to the third person | A conflictual marriage becomes more harmonious after the birth of a child (parents shift focus of anxieties from one another to child) |
| **Unstable twosome stabilized by removing a third** | The removed person had been fomenting conflict through side-taking | Conflict in a twosome is reduced when the two people avoid a third person who consistently took sides |

## The Diffusing Capacity of Triangles

### How Triangles Manage Tension

The diffusing capacity of a triangle is its ability to spread and shift anxiety so that no single relationship is overwhelmed. This capacity depends on:

1. **Level of differentiation** — The higher the differentiation of the participants, the less the triangle is needed for stability and the more each person can maintain emotional autonomy
2. **Level of anxiety** — The higher the anxiety, the more actively the triangle shifts and the more likely it overflows into adjacent triangles
3. **Flexibility of the shifting** — In a well-functioning triangle, tension shifts fluidly; in a rigid triangle, one relationship chronically carries the tension (becoming symptomatic)

### When Diffusion Works

Triangles allow relationship systems to:
- Absorb shock from acute stressors without any single relationship breaking down
- Distribute chronic anxiety across multiple relationships so it remains below symptomatic levels
- Maintain overall system stability even when individual relationships are strained

### When Diffusion Fails

The diffusing capacity is exceeded when:
- Anxiety is too high for one triangle to contain → **overflow into interlocking triangles**
- The triangle becomes rigid — tension crystallizes in one relationship and cannot shift
- All three relationships become highly conflictual simultaneously (system breakdown)

## Interlocking Triangles

### The Overflow Process

It is not always possible for a person to shift the forces in a triangle. When it is not possible, the anxiety **spreads to other triangles in an interlocking fashion.**

**Clinical Example — Interlocking Family Triangles:**

1. A father withdraws in response to his wife's effort to involve him with their acting-out son
2. This exacerbates the tension between mother and son
3. The mother communicates her anxiety and frustration to another child, involving that child in the tension
4. Conflict erupts between the two siblings when the one triangled in attempts to get the other to "behave"
5. Meanwhile, mother achieves an outside position

When one triangle was not available, the tension spilled over into another.

**Alternative Scenario:**

1. The father, in response to his wife's distress, gets into conflict with the son
2. As tension mounts between father and son, the wife withdraws
3. The father, now that his wife is temporarily unavailable, involves another child in the process
4. Conflict shifts from the father-son relationship to between the siblings

### Properties of Interlocking Triangles

- In a calm family, anxiety can be contained mostly in **one central triangle**
- Under stress, anxiety spreads to other family triangles and to **triangles outside the family** in work and social systems
- Interlocking triangles can significantly reduce anxiety in a family's central triangle by spreading it across a much wider network
- The more poorly differentiated the system, the more the interlocking process is needed and the more active it becomes

### Triangles Beyond the Family

Triangling extends beyond the nuclear family to include:
- Extended family members (in-laws, grandparents)
- Friends, colleagues, therapists, clergy
- Institutions (school, church, workplace)
- Substances (alcohol, drugs, food)
- Issues and causes (politics, religion, ideology)
- Activities (work, hobbies, affairs)

Any third entity that can absorb anxiety from a twosome can function as a point in a triangle.

## Triangles and Differentiation

The intensity of the triangling process varies among families and in the same family over time because **triangles are a product of the undifferentiation in the human process:**

- The lower the level of differentiation, the more important the role of triangling for preserving emotional stability
- If anxiety is very low, even in a poorly differentiated family the three members of a triangle can function as emotionally separate individuals — but the stress must be very low for this to occur
- **In an extremely well-differentiated system, people can maintain their emotional separateness even when highly stressed**
- If people can maintain their emotional autonomy, triangling is minimal, and the system's stability does not depend on it

This means that triangling is not inherently pathological — it is a natural feature of all emotional systems. It becomes problematic when the system's stability **depends** on rigid triangling patterns, when one person is chronically fixed in a position (especially the scapegoated outsider or the symptomatic absorber), or when the system cannot tolerate any change in the triangle configuration.

## Governing Variables

Bowen identified two main variables governing triangle intensity in the nuclear family:

1. **Degree of emotional cutoff from the extended family** — Greater isolation from extended family intensifies nuclear family triangles because there are fewer people available to absorb and diffuse anxiety (see [Emotional Cutoff](emotional-cutoff.md))
2. **Level of anxiety** — Symptoms are less intense when anxiety is low, more intense when high (see [Chronic Anxiety](chronic-anxiety.md))

## Clinical Applications

### Assessment

1. **Map the triangles** in the family system — identify the central triangle and the most active interlocking triangles
2. **Identify insider/outsider positions** and how they shift with changes in anxiety
3. **Track the movement of tension** — where does anxiety enter the system and how does it travel through the triangle network?
4. **Identify rigid triangles** — which triangles have become fixed, with one person chronically carrying the tension?
5. **Assess triangling beyond the family** — are work, social, or institutional triangles involved in the family process?
6. **Look for cross-generational triangles** — are current triangles replaying unresolved conflicts from previous generations?

### Therapeutic Approach: Modifying Triangles

Bowen stated: **"Modify the triangle and you have modified the system."** Therapeutic change operates through triangle modification:

- **Any one person** changing their part of the pattern can shift the triangle's automatic functioning
- The triangled person can choose not to absorb the anxiety
- The anxious person can process their own anxiety rather than transmitting it
- **The key move is working on self in the original two-person relationship** rather than displacing to a third

### The Therapist's Position

The therapist must manage self to avoid being triangled into the family's emotional process:

- The therapist is automatically pulled to take sides, assign blame, or feel more sympathetic to one family member
- A therapist who takes sides has become part of the triangle and lost therapeutic leverage
- The therapist's calm, non-reactive presence interrupts the automatic anxiety circuit
- **Maintaining a systems perspective** — observing function rather than assigning motive — is the therapist's primary discipline

### De-Triangling

The process of de-triangling involves:

1. **Awareness** — recognizing one's position and function in the triangle
2. **Emotional neutrality** — maintaining contact with both other members without taking sides
3. **Self-focus** — working on one's own emotional reactivity rather than trying to change the other two
4. **Staying in contact** — remaining emotionally connected to both parties rather than withdrawing (which would simply create a new triangle)

De-triangling is one of the most difficult and most powerful therapeutic moves in Bowen theory. It requires sustained effort and high tolerance for the anxiety that arises when a familiar triangle pattern is disrupted.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---------------|------------|
| Triangles are always pathological | Triangles are a natural fact of all emotional systems; they become problematic only when rigid or when one person is chronically impaired |
| The third person is always a victim | All three participants contribute to the triangle; the third person often actively inserts themselves |
| Triangles are intentional manipulation | Most triangling occurs without conscious awareness — it is automatic emotional process, not strategic behavior |
| Removing the third person solves the problem | It may destabilize the system further; the underlying two-person tension remains unresolved |
| Understanding "why" someone triangles is therapeutic | Focus on function (what, how, when, where), not motive (why) — "why" pulls the observer out of a systems frame |
