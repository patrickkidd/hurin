# Family Evaluation

> Source: Kerr & Bowen, *Family Evaluation* (1988), Chapters 7–10; supplemented by clinical methodology throughout the text

## Overview

Family evaluation in the Bowen framework is the process of assessing a family's emotional functioning across generations in order to understand the context out of which clinical symptoms have developed. It is not a diagnostic procedure in the conventional sense — it does not seek to classify the symptomatic individual's disorder into a DSM category. Instead, it seeks to answer a fundamentally different question: **"How has the balance of the emotional system — within this person and within this person's relationship network — been disturbed?"**

The family evaluation rests on the premise that clinical symptoms are products of the emotional system and that understanding the emotional system requires looking beyond the individual to the family as the unit of observation. The evaluation integrates data from multiple levels: biological, psychological, relational, and multigenerational.

---

## Principles of Family Evaluation

### 1. The Family as the Unit

The individual patient is not evaluated in isolation. The presenting symptom is understood as a manifestation of emotional process in the family system. This does not mean the individual's biology or psychology is ignored — it means those factors are understood in the context of the relationship system.

### 2. Process Over Content

The evaluator listens for patterns of emotional functioning — who pursues, who distances, who overfunctions, who underfunctions, who gets triangled — rather than getting absorbed in the specific complaints, stories, or explanations family members offer. Content changes constantly; process repeats.

### 3. Facts Over Interpretations

The evaluation prioritizes factual data — dates, events, health histories, geographic moves, occupational trajectories — over subjective interpretations of motives or feelings. Facts are less distorted by the emotional process than interpretations. A comprehensive factual history reveals patterns that subjective accounts obscure.

### 4. Multigenerational Scope

A thorough evaluation includes at least three generations, ideally more. The patterns visible in the nuclear family are understood as products of emotional processes that have been transmitted across generations. Without the multigenerational view, the current family's patterns appear to exist in a vacuum.

### 5. The Therapist's Own Differentiation

The evaluator's ability to maintain a systems perspective depends on managing their own emotional reactivity. An anxious therapist who takes sides, identifies with the symptom bearer, or gets drawn into the family's content has lost the systems view. The therapist must function as a **differentiated observer** — in contact with the family but not fused with it.

## The Family Evaluation Interview

### Setting the Frame

The initial interview typically includes both spouses (or the primary relationship dyad). The therapist establishes several ground rules that reflect Bowen theory principles:

- **Each person speaks for self.** "I think..." not "We feel..." or "He always..."
- **No interrupting.** Each person gets uninterrupted time to describe their view of the situation.
- **No telling the other what to think or feel.** The focus is on each person's own functioning, not on changing the other.
- **The therapist asks questions; the family provides data.** This is not a free-form discussion or an airing of grievances.

### The Therapist's Stance

The Bowen therapist functions differently from therapists in most other modalities:

| Conventional Therapy | Bowen Evaluation |
|---|---|
| Empathic attunement to feelings | Calm, factual inquiry into process |
| Focus on the presenting problem | Focus on the relationship system that produced the problem |
| Therapeutic alliance with the patient | Therapeutic contact with the system — no alliance with any one member against another |
| Interpretation of unconscious dynamics | Description of observable patterns |
| Treatment plan for the identified patient | Assessment of the family's emotional system and the mechanisms producing the symptom |

The therapist maintains a position that Bowen called the "research stance" — genuinely curious about how the system works, not invested in a particular outcome, and managing their own anxiety well enough to think clearly in the presence of the family's anxiety.

### Interview Structure

A comprehensive family evaluation typically unfolds over several sessions and covers the following domains:

#### A. The Presenting Problem

- What brought the family to seek help now?
- When did the symptom first appear? What was happening in the family at that time?
- How has the symptom evolved? What makes it better or worse?
- Who is most concerned about the symptom? Who is least concerned?
- What has been tried? What helped? What didn't?

**Systems question (from George Engel):** "Why does this patient have this disease *now*?"

#### B. The Nuclear Family Emotional Process

- What are the four patterns' relative activity levels? (See [Nuclear Family Emotional System](nuclear-family-emotional-system.md))
  - **Marital conflict:** How do the spouses handle disagreements? What happens when anxiety rises?
  - **Overfunctioning/underfunctioning:** Who makes decisions? Who adapts? Has this shifted over time?
  - **Emotional distance:** How much real contact exists between spouses? Between parents and children?
  - **Child focus:** Is parental anxiety focused on a particular child? How is that focus manifested?
- How is the family's undifferentiation distributed across these patterns?
- What has changed recently in the distribution?

#### C. The Extended Family System

- What is each spouse's relationship with their family of origin?
- Degree of [emotional cutoff](emotional-cutoff.md) — physical distance, emotional distance, or both?
- Which extended family relationships are supportive? Which are conflictual?
- How does each spouse's position in their family of origin influence their current functioning?

#### D. The Multigenerational History

This is the most distinctive element of Bowen evaluation. The therapist gathers detailed factual information about at least three generations (ideally more) to construct a **family diagram** (genogram). Key data for each family member includes:

- Birth date, death date, cause of death
- Occupational history and educational history
- Health history — physical, emotional, and social dysfunction
- Marital history (marriages, divorces, separations)
- Reproductive history
- History of geographic relocations — toward goals or away from problems?
- Significant life events and their timing

#### E. Sibling Position

- What is each spouse's functional position among siblings?
- Does current marital functioning reflect [sibling position](differentiation.md) profiles? (Toman's research)
- Which child in the current family occupies which functional position?
- How does sibling position relate to the family projection process?

#### F. Stressor Inventory

- What stressors has the family encountered recently? (Both "positive" and "negative" life events add to the anxiety load)
- What is the cumulative stress burden?
- How does the current stress level compare to the family's baseline?
- Are there anniversary reactions or temporal patterns to symptom flare-ups?

## The Family Diagram: The Central Clinical Tool

### What It Is

The family diagram (genogram) is a structured, multigenerational map of a family's factual history and relationship patterns. It is the primary tool of Bowen family evaluation — the equivalent of the physical examination in medicine or the balance sheet in accounting.

### What It Contains

A comprehensive family diagram includes:

**Structural elements:**
- All family members across at least three generations
- Birth and death dates
- Marriages, divorces, remarriages
- Children (including miscarriages, stillbirths, adoptions)
- Sibling order

**Functional elements:**
- Health histories (physical, emotional, social)
- Occupational and educational trajectories
- Geographic moves and their motivations
- Cutoffs and reconnections
- Nodal events (deaths, divorces, major illnesses, moves)

**Relationship process indicators:**
- Which patterns predominate in each nuclear family unit
- Where anxiety is bound (conflict, spouse dysfunction, child focus)
- Multigenerational trends in functioning
- Triangles — who is triangled with whom across generations

### How It Is Used Clinically

The family diagram serves multiple clinical functions:

#### 1. Pattern Recognition

When the diagram is "examined as a whole," marked differences in the functioning of individuals and nuclear family units are seen to be **linked to trends in functioning that develop over a number of generations.** The diagram reveals:

- Downward trends: successive generations with decreasing differentiation, increasing symptom severity
- Upward trends: successive generations with increasing differentiation, better functioning
- Lateral variation: siblings in the same generation functioning at markedly different levels
- Repetitive patterns: the same type of dysfunction (alcoholism, depression, cutoff) recurring across generations

#### 2. Contextualizing the Presenting Problem

The presenting symptom is placed in the context of:
- The multigenerational emotional process that produced this family's particular vulnerability
- The specific patterns of emotional functioning active in the nuclear family
- The current stress load relative to the family's adaptive capacity
- The timing of symptom onset relative to nodal events

#### 3. Predicting Vulnerability

Based on the multigenerational assessment, the clinician can identify:
- Which family members are most vulnerable to symptom development
- What types of symptoms are most likely given the family's pattern
- What stressors are most likely to trigger symptom emergence
- What multigenerational trends are operating

#### 4. Guiding Intervention

The diagram guides the therapist's focus:
- Which relationships are most important to address?
- Where are the [triangles](triangles.md) that are maintaining the current symptom?
- What cutoffs from extended family are intensifying nuclear family anxiety?
- What level of intervention is realistic given the family's level of differentiation?

### Assessment of Overall Functioning

Using the criteria from the family diagram, each individual can be assessed on a continuum between two extremes:

**Stable functioning pole:**
- Fairly long-lived
- Full advantage taken of educational and occupational opportunities
- Lives unencumbered by serious physical, emotional, or social dysfunction
- Marriages intact
- Spouses and children function at similar levels
- Geographic moves motivated by going *toward* goals rather than *away from* problems

**Unstable functioning pole:**
- Lack motivation or consistently squander opportunities
- Lives encumbered by serious physical, emotional, or social dysfunction
- Relationships unstable
- Often cut off from formerly important people
- Geographic relocations are frenzied attempts to find solutions to old problems in new places

Family members at gradations between these extremes have stable and unstable aspects of their functioning. The higher the level of functioning, the greater the proportion of stable aspects.

## Evaluating the Nuclear Family Patterns

### Identifying the Predominant Pattern

The evaluator determines which pattern or patterns of emotional functioning are most active:

**Indicators of marital conflict predominance:**
- Spouses report chronic disagreement on multiple topics
- Anxiety consistently increases marital tension rather than producing individual symptoms
- Children are relatively symptom-free despite marital turmoil
- Conflict intensity tracks with external stress levels

**Indicators of spouse dysfunction predominance:**
- One spouse is chronically symptomatic (physical, emotional, or social)
- The other spouse is functioning well, often appearing unusually competent
- The marital relationship may appear stable — even harmonious — despite one spouse's impairment
- The overfunctioning spouse may describe the underfunctioning one as "the patient"

**Indicators of child impairment predominance:**
- One or more children are symptomatic while parents and marriage appear intact
- Parents are preoccupied with a child's problems
- Parental anxiety visibly escalates when the focused-on child's symptoms worsen
- Siblings of the focused-on child may function notably well

**Indicators of emotional distance predominance:**
- The couple reports little conflict but also little real connection
- Both spouses manage anxiety independently
- The relationship appears stable but is actually frozen
- Symptoms may emerge when distance is disrupted (retirement, illness, children leaving)

### Assessing Pattern Flexibility

A critical clinical assessment: **How rigid or flexible is the family's pattern distribution?**

- **Rigid:** Anxiety is consistently bound in one pattern, one person, one relationship. Prognosis for the focused-on member is worse. Example: unrelenting focus on one child → schizophrenic-level impairment.
- **Flexible:** Anxiety shifts among patterns and family members over time. No single person absorbs a chronic, disproportionate share. Example: mother is physically ill during one period of high anxiety; during the next, a child develops academic problems; during the next, father has a depression. No one person is chronically impaired.

### Tracking Pattern Shifts

Patterns can shift:
- Over the lifecycle of the family (early marriage → child-rearing → empty nest)
- In response to changing stress levels
- When a family member enters or exits the system (birth, death, departure, return)
- When cutoff relationships are restored or new cutoffs occur

The evaluator tracks these shifts to understand how the family's emotional process has evolved and how the current symptom picture developed.

## The Multigenerational Assessment

### What to Look For

When examining the family diagram across generations:

1. **Trends in differentiation:** Is there a progressive decrease (or increase) in functioning across generations? How rapid is the change?

2. **Pattern replication:** Do the same patterns of emotional functioning recur? Does marital conflict in one generation become spouse dysfunction in the next? Does the family projection process target the same sibling position across generations?

3. **Nodal events and their ripple effects:** Deaths, divorces, geographic moves — how did these events affect the family's anxiety and patterns? Did symptom onset in the current generation follow a nodal event in a previous generation?

4. **Cutoff patterns:** Where has emotional cutoff occurred across generations? What is the relationship between cutoff and symptom development?

5. **Quantum jumps vs. gradual trends:** Did functioning change dramatically over a few generations, or was the change gradual? How many generations contributed to the current level of functioning?

### Evaluating Multigenerational Trends

From the source material, key principles for multigenerational assessment:

- A person who functions at an extremely unstable level is usually the product of a multigenerational trend — their parents' functioning was somewhat unstable, their grandparents' somewhat less so
- A person who functions at an extremely stable level is usually the product of a corresponding upward trend
- Quantum jumps (marked change in three generations) are possible but uncommon
- More typical is mild to moderate change over four to five generations
- Marked decreases in functioning most commonly require five to ten generations
- Pronounced increases in functioning are similarly gradual

### The 62/1000 Principle

Each nuclear family is the product of:
- **62 nuclear families** in the five generations that precede it
- **Over 1,000 nuclear families** in the nine generations that precede it

This means the possible range in functioning of nuclear families in a five-to-ten-generation span can be quite marked. The family diagram, even when limited to three or four generations, provides a window into this much larger multigenerational process.

## Differential Assessment: Systems vs. Individual

### The Systems Question

For every presenting problem, the evaluator asks: **"How much of this symptom is a product of this person's position in the emotional system, and how much reflects individual factors (genetics, physiology, temperament) that would be present regardless of the relational context?"**

This is not an either/or question. A systems model assumes that individual and relational factors are interrelated and mutually reinforcing. But the relative contribution of each informs the treatment approach:

- If the symptom is primarily maintained by the family emotional process, shifting that process (through [differentiation](differentiation.md) work) may be the most effective intervention
- If the symptom has a strong biological component (e.g., insulin-dependent diabetes), medical management is essential — but the family emotional process still influences disease management, compliance, and overall functioning
- Most clinical situations involve both — the question is one of emphasis and sequencing

### Evaluating "Why Now?"

Engel's question — "Why does this patient have this disease now?" — requires correlating:

1. **Biological vulnerability** (genetics, pre-existing conditions, age-related factors)
2. **Cumulative stress/anxiety load** (recent events, chronic stressors, anniversary reactions)
3. **Pattern activation** (which emotional patterns have intensified recently)
4. **System balance disruption** (what changed in the relationship system that tipped the balance)

The convergence of these factors at a particular moment explains why *this* person developed *this* symptom *now* — even though the vulnerability may have existed for years.

## Clinical Applications of the Evaluation

### Treatment Planning

The family evaluation directly informs treatment decisions:

| Evaluation Finding | Treatment Implication |
|---|---|
| Symptom maintained primarily by family projection process | Work with parents on their own functioning rather than treating child individually |
| Symptom correlates with emotional cutoff from extended family | Coach reconnection with extended family to reduce nuclear family anxiety load |
| Overfunctioning/underfunctioning reciprocity driving spouse dysfunction | Help the overfunctioner stop doing for the other; support the underfunctioner in doing for self |
| High chronic anxiety with rigid pattern distribution | Lower the anxiety first (through therapist's calm presence, psychoeducation) before attempting pattern change |
| Multigenerational trend toward decreasing differentiation | Work on differentiation of self in the family of origin — not just the nuclear family |

### Ongoing Assessment

The family evaluation is not a one-time event. The family diagram is a living document, updated as new information emerges. Throughout therapy, the clinician continues to:

- Track changes in pattern activity
- Monitor anxiety levels
- Observe shifts in triangles
- Note the effects of life events on the system
- Assess progress in differentiation efforts

### The Family Diagram in Therapy

The diagram itself becomes a therapeutic tool:

1. **Psychoeducation:** Showing the family their patterns on the diagram helps them see the *process* rather than blaming individuals
2. **Depersonalization:** When a parent sees that the projection pattern existed in their own parents' generation, the guilt and blame associated with their child's symptoms can diminish
3. **Motivation:** Seeing multigenerational trends can motivate work on differentiation — the client can see where the process is heading if nothing changes
4. **Tracking progress:** Changes in functioning can be mapped on the diagram over time

## Common Evaluation Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Systems Correction |
|---|---|
| **Accepting the identified patient framing** — evaluating only the symptomatic person | Always evaluate the relationship system; the symptom bearer is not necessarily the person most in need of change |
| **Getting absorbed in content** — drawn into the family's stories and explanations | Track process: who does what in response to whom, when, and in what sequence |
| **Taking sides** — aligning with one family member against another | Maintain the research stance; each person's behavior is part of a reciprocal system |
| **Ignoring multigenerational data** — treating the current nuclear family as if it exists in isolation | The current patterns are products of multigenerational transmission; gather three or more generations |
| **Cause-and-effect thinking** — looking for the single "cause" of the symptom | Multiple factors interact; the question is not "what caused this" but "how has the system balance been disturbed" |
| **Emotional reactivity to the family's anxiety** — the therapist becomes anxious, loses perspective | The therapist's own [differentiation](differentiation.md) is the primary therapeutic instrument |
| **Focusing on the symptom rather than the system** — treating the symptom without addressing the patterns that produced it | The symptom is a signal of system imbalance, not the problem itself |
