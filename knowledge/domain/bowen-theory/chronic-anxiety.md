# Chronic Anxiety

> Source: Kerr & Bowen, *Family Evaluation* (1988), Chapter 5; supplemented by Gilbert (2006)

## Overview

Differentiation of self and chronic anxiety are the **two principal variables** in family systems theory that explain level of functioning. Differentiation describes a person's adaptive capacity; chronic anxiety describes the strain on that capacity. Symptom development is always a function of the interaction between these two variables — it depends on both the amount of stress and the adaptiveness of the individual or family to stress.

- Highly adaptive (well-differentiated) people and families require considerable stress to trigger symptoms.
- Poorly adaptive (poorly differentiated) people and families can be symptom-free if chronic anxiety is low.

This means there is no fixed "breaking point" for a person or family — functioning is always contextual, always relational to both internal capacity and external/systemic pressure.

## Definitions

### Anxiety as Biological Process

Anxiety is the response of an organism to a threat, real or imagined. It is assumed to be present in some form in **all living things**. The physiological systems involved in anxiety grew more complex as evolution progressed, but at every level of the phylogenetic tree organisms show increased emotional reactivity in response to environmental threats.

Emotional reactivity and anxiety are not easily distinguished and are used interchangeably in family systems theory. Increased anxiety manifests as increased degrees of various types of emotional reactivity — gaze aversion, aggression, flight — but anxiety itself can be considered a type of emotional reactivity with its own unique manifestations:

- **Subjective manifestations:** Heightened sense of awareness, fear of impending disaster, rumination, sense of being overwhelmed
- **Objective manifestations:** Increased responsiveness, restlessness, autonomic nervous system changes (increased heart rate, blood pressure, cortisol)

Reactivity is manifested along a continuum that ranges from **hyperactivity** (the extreme is behavioral frenzy) to **hypoactivity** (the extreme is behavioral paralysis). Like most biological processes, too much or too little reactivity reduces an organism's adaptiveness. Anxiety has critical adaptive functions — it mobilizes the organism for action in the face of real danger — but becomes pathological when chronically elevated or poorly regulated.

### Acute vs. Chronic Anxiety

This distinction is one of Bowen theory's most clinically useful contributions:

| Dimension | Acute Anxiety | Chronic Anxiety |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Trigger** | Real threats | Imagined threats |
| **Time course** | Time-limited | Not time-limited |
| **Adaptation** | People usually adapt successfully | Often strains or exceeds adaptive capacity |
| **Orientation** | Fear of *what is* | Fear of *what might be* |
| **Basis** | More innate/hardwired | More learned |
| **Physiology** | Adrenaline-mediated (adrenal medulla) | Cortisol-mediated (adrenal cortex) |
| **Long-term effects** | Self-resolving | Weight gain, immune suppression, ulcers, cognitive decline |
| **Role of learning** | Inborn elements dominate | Learning plays a more important role |

While everyone experiences both acute and chronic anxiety, the **difference between people in the amount of chronic anxiety they experience is based primarily on learned responses** — patterns absorbed during the developmental years within the family emotional system.

### Chronic Anxiety as a Self-Sustaining System

Chronic anxiety is most accurately conceptualized not as a reaction to a specific cause, but as **a system or process of actions and reactions that, once triggered, quickly provides its own momentum and becomes largely independent of the initial triggering stimuli.** This is a critical distinction:

- Specific events or issues are usually the principal generators of *acute* anxiety
- The principal generators of *chronic* anxiety are **people's reactions to a disturbance in the balance of a relationship system**
- Real or anticipated events (retirement, a child leaving home, pregnancy, job loss) may initially disturb the balance, but once disturbed, chronic anxiety is propagated more by **people's reactions to the disturbance** than by reactions to the event itself

Chronic anxiety has manifestations on levels ranging from **intracellular systems to societal processes**. It is influenced by many things but is not caused by any one thing. This self-sustaining quality is what makes chronic anxiety so difficult to interrupt — it is not anchored to any single cause and therefore cannot be resolved by eliminating any single factor.

### The Central Principle: Relationship Reactivity Drives Chronic Anxiety

> "When people can maintain comfortable contact with emotionally significant others they are more likely to adapt successfully to events that are potentially stressful."

This is the key insight: **it is not the event that creates chronic anxiety, but the disruption of relationship equilibrium that follows the event, and the chain of reactivity that follows the disruption.**

## Mechanisms: How Chronic Anxiety Operates

### Relationship Equilibrium and Its Disturbance

The central mechanism of chronic anxiety operates through relationship systems:

1. A relationship system exists in equilibrium (however imperfect)
2. An event or change disturbs that equilibrium
3. People react to the disturbance with increased emotional reactivity
4. The reactivity of each person intensifies the reactivity of others
5. The chain reaction generates chronic anxiety that far exceeds what the original event warranted
6. The system may not be able to restore equilibrium without introducing a chronic symptom

**Clinical Example — Pregnancy:**

A couple's relationship is in harmony until the wife gets pregnant. The anticipated birth disturbs the emotional equilibrium:

1. The mother feels overloaded by anticipated responsibility and wants to lean more on her husband for emotional support
2. The husband gets reactive to his wife's neediness and becomes critical, pulling away
3. His distancing isolates the wife, which further increases her anxiety and yearning for support
4. Her anxiety remains high for months until the family system establishes a new equilibrium that includes the child

Had the husband and wife not gotten so reactive to one another, each could have adapted to the pregnancy more successfully. The key generator of chronic anxiety was not the pregnancy itself but the **couple's reactivity to each other** in the context of the pregnancy.

**Clinical Example — Child Leaving Home:**

A child's departure may result in changes in the parents' relationship. The child may have functioned as a buffer for parental conflicts (see [Triangles](triangles.md)). After the child leaves, the previous equilibrium is disturbed and the parents' dissatisfactions and frustrations with one another intensify. The increased reactivity of the parents to one another generates more anxiety than their reactions to the absence of the child. The event (child leaving) triggered the disturbance, but the chronic anxiety is sustained by the relationship process.

### Symptom as Stabilizer

In a poorly differentiated system, after equilibrium has been disturbed **it may not be possible to restore equilibrium without the introduction of a chronic symptom.** The symptom becomes a feature of the family's adaptation to internal and external stresses.

**Clinical Example — Post-Partum Alcohol Use:**

Following the birth of a baby, the reactivity between husband and wife precludes reduction of anxiety through support provided by the marital relationship. In lieu of the relationship, drinking provides some relief from anxiety. If both spouses assume that the problem is her drinking and not their relationship, this takes pressure off the marriage and allows equilibrium to be restored.

It is "easier" — providing an illusion of marital harmony attractive to both — for each spouse to define the problem as drinking rather than as the relationship. The wife's drinking is an outgrowth of the way *both* spouses function, not a product of there being more defects in the wife's character than the husband's.

**Key systems insight:** The symptom bearer is not necessarily the person with the most individual pathology. The symptom is a product of the system, and often serves a stabilizing function for the system.

### The Infectious Nature of Anxiety

Anxiety "rubs off" on people — it is transmitted and absorbed without thinking. This absorption appears to be based on:

1. **Physiological programming/conditioning** — prolonged association with anxious others conditions one's own physiological responses through what appears to be an osmotic process
2. **Incorporation of attitudes and beliefs** that foster anxiety in oneself or others
   - "I am inferior" creates anxiety for oneself
   - "I am the greatest" can create problems for others

Due to this infectious quality and the way anxiety permeates the atmosphere, a child tends to develop a baseline level of chronic anxiety close to what is average for the nuclear family in which he grows up:

- If raised in a well-differentiated family → less chronic anxiety present → less incorporated
- If raised in a poorly differentiated family → vulnerable to absorbing much more

**Parents cannot truly "protect" their children from the transmission of anxieties.** The process is too wired into the way parents function, and children are highly prone to picking up anxiety. **Trying to protect children from one's own problems is actually one of the main ways problems are transmitted from one generation to the next.** This is because the "protection" itself is anxiously driven — the parent's worry about the child absorbing anxiety *is itself* anxiety that the child absorbs.

### Anxiety and the Thinking-Feeling-Emotional System

A person's response to any stimulus operates simultaneously on three levels (see [Individuality and Togetherness](differentiation.md)):

1. **Intellectual system** — Thinking, reasoning, reflection
2. **Feeling system** — Subjective emotional experience
3. **Emotional system** — Automatic, biologically driven reactivity

When these responses are reasonably balanced, one level does not overwhelm the others. But when a person is emotionally involved in a situation, the thinking response can be overwhelmed by intense feeling and emotional responses. These feeling and emotional reactions occur so quickly that they appear to **envelop and modify the thinking response**. The orderliness and breadth of thinking are changed under pressure from the emotional system. Sometimes people are aware that their thinking is altered; more often the subtleness of the process precludes awareness.

This is the mechanism by which chronic anxiety degrades functioning:

- **Anxiety → overwhelmed thinking → tunnel vision → more anxiety**
- The less able a person is to distinguish emotional, feeling, and thinking responses, the greater the likelihood of "explaining too much on the basis of too little"
- Ruminations foster more anxiety, which fosters more ruminations — it is not a process one "shuts off" easily
- People lose the ability to observe the *process* of their relationships and instead fixate on emotionally charged details, blowing them out of proportion

**Clinical Example — The Pursue/Withdraw Cycle:**

A wife notices her husband has withdrawn. She not only observes this intellectually but also feels threatened by it and has an emotionally driven urge to pursue. If she could think objectively, she could observe that his urge to withdraw and her desire for more contact are feeding on one another. But since her feeling and emotional reactions are hampering clear thinking, she blames his withdrawal on indifference and obsesses about her own inadequacies. This is tunnel vision — certain details that are only part of a larger process are blown out of proportion and used to explain everything.

### The "Why" Trap

A common manifestation of anxiety-driven tunnel vision is people incessantly asking one another **"why"** they behave the way they do. When one person asks the other "Why do you do what you do?" focus on the relationship process is **immediately lost**:

- The question assumes the cause of the person's behavior exists *within* that person
- It shifts the "locus of control" from the relationship to one person
- One person does not withdraw *because* the other pursues, any more than the other pursues *because* one withdraws
- It is a process that transcends a "why" explanation contained within either individual

> "An anxious family elevates a facet of a problem to the cause of the problem; if a therapist falls into the same trap, his potential value to the family is lost."

## Individual Variation in Chronic Anxiety

### The Differentiation-Anxiety Relationship

The average level of chronic anxiety of a person and of a nuclear family unit parallels the basic level of differentiation. **The lower the level of basic differentiation, the higher the average level of chronic anxiety.** This component has little to do with current life situation but is learned during the developmental years and carried through life. This learning occurs on several levels:

- The seemingly osmotic absorption of parental anxieties
- The incorporation of subjectively determined attitudes that create anxiety (e.g., low self-esteem)

A nuclear family unit also has an average level of chronic anxiety which is a product of the emotional makeup of family members and the way they affect one another.

However, differentiation and chronic anxiety are **distinct processes**:

- A well-differentiated person under extreme stress can have very high anxiety
- A poorly differentiated person in a calm environment can be symptom-free
- **Symptom development = f(differentiation level × chronic anxiety level)**

The most fundamental explanation for why chronic anxiety increases as differentiation decreases: **the less a person has grown away (emotionally separated) from his family, the more anxiety he has about being on his own and assuming responsibility for himself.** Some people deal with this by never leaving home; others leave and "pretend" to have grown up. The degree of pretend is betrayed by the amount of anxiety associated with trying to be a responsible adult.

### Variation by Range on the Differentiation Scale

**0–25 Range:**
- Little emotional separation from family of origin
- Often an obvious "bundle" of anxiety
- Distress seems almost biological in nature, far beyond the reach of "Why are you so anxious?"
- Little confidence in ability to take care of themselves and manage their lives
- Highly vulnerable to "soaking up" anxiety from the environment
- Highly prone to "infecting" others with their anxiety
- Do best in a calm environment, yet play a part in creating an anxious environment around them
- So little to counterbalance the feeling process that anxieties easily escalate to high levels
- Anxiety fosters ruminations which foster more anxiety which foster more ruminations
- Many abnormal biochemical and physiological findings in schizophrenic individuals may reflect psychological and physiological adjustments made by poorly differentiated people in response to high chronic anxiety

**25–50 Range:**
- Psychological components of chronic anxiety are usually well articulated (a less intense version of what operates lower on the scale)
- Common anxiety generators:
  - Dealing with uncertainty
  - Anticipating the worst
  - Mulling over whether one is approved of, accepted, or rejected
  - Being preoccupied with what "should" or "ought" to be done
  - Preoccupation with one's inadequacy
  - Feeling overloaded by responsibility
- The more one depends on reinforcement from others, the more obsessed one becomes about others' attitudes toward them

**50+ Range:**
- Greater capacity to manage anxiety through thinking and principled action
- Less dependent on relationship validation
- Anxiety present but less likely to become chronic or to overwhelm functioning
- Better able to maintain comfortable contact during stressful periods
- Can observe the difference between emotional, feeling, and thinking responses

### Multigenerational Origins of Variation

The variation in chronic anxiety among individuals and families can only be fully understood by examining the multigenerational family history (see [Multigenerational Transmission Process](multigenerational-transmission.md)). In every family, the level of chronic anxiety shows a **gradual increase through the generations in some branches and a decrease in others:**

1. **Children from the same nuclear family** have different degrees of emotional separation from parents
2. **People marry spouses** with equivalent degrees of emotional separation from their families
3. **Children of these new marriages** have unequal degrees of emotional separation and, in turn, marry people like themselves
4. **This process repeats** generation after generation

Eventually this creates:
- Segments of family where people have little emotional separation from one another → high chronic anxiety
- Segments where people are reasonably differentiated → low chronic anxiety
- Segments reflecting gradations between these extremes

As the generations pass, some branches become more governed by automatic emotional reactivity and subjectivity than others. The increased undifferentiation means that the functioning of family members is more relationship-dependent — **a dependence that spawns chronic anxiety.**

### Nuclear Family Imprinting

An individual growing up in a given nuclear family is "imprinted" with a level of chronic anxiety characteristic of that branch. But the average level developed by children in the same nuclear family is **not equal** because all siblings do not separate emotionally from parents to the same extent:

- The child **most caught up** in the family emotional problem separates the least, is the most relationship-dependent, and "inherits" the most chronic anxiety
- The child **least involved** in the family problem separates the most, is the least relationship-dependent, and inherits the least anxiety

So the most emotionally involved child in a very poorly differentiated nuclear family (the outcome of many generations of gradual change) will absorb and generate large amounts of chronic anxiety, whereas the least involved child in a well-differentiated family (also the outcome of many generations) will absorb and generate a low level.

This process is mediated by the [Family Projection Process](family-projection-process.md) and operates through [Triangles](triangles.md).

## Chronic Anxiety in Society

### Societal Regression

Bowen extended the concept of chronic anxiety beyond the family to society as a whole (see [Societal Emotional Process](societal-regression.md)). Just as a family can be more or less anxious, so can an entire society. Societal chronic anxiety increases during periods of:

- Population pressure and resource scarcity
- Rapid social change and disruption of traditional structures
- Perceived threats (economic, military, environmental)
- Erosion of differentiated leadership

When societal anxiety rises, the same patterns observed in families appear at the societal level:
- Increased togetherness pressure ("think like us or you're the enemy")
- Decreased tolerance for individuality and dissent
- Short-term, reactive decision-making replacing long-term, principled thinking
- Blaming and scapegoating of vulnerable groups
- Proliferation of "experts" offering emotionally appealing but intellectually shallow solutions

### The Anxiety Spiral in Groups

In any group — family, organization, or society — chronic anxiety follows a characteristic escalation pattern:

1. A disturbance triggers reactivity
2. Reactivity in one member triggers reactivity in others
3. People focus on surface-level "causes" rather than the systemic process
4. Solutions aimed at symptoms rather than process generate more anxiety
5. The system becomes increasingly governed by emotional reactivity rather than thoughtful response

This is why Bowen argued that **the most important thing a therapist, leader, or citizen can do is manage their own anxiety** rather than trying to fix others. A non-anxious presence in a system has a calming effect that ripples outward, just as anxiety does.

### Chronic Anxiety and Group Process

The intensity of chronic anxiety in any group is significantly governed by the interplay of individuality and togetherness. When chronic anxiety is high:

- The togetherness force intensifies — pressure to conform increases
- Individuality is experienced as threatening to the group
- People sacrifice their own thinking to maintain relationship harmony
- The group becomes more susceptible to tunnel vision and reactive decision-making
- Leaders who maintain a differentiated stance are often attacked or excluded

When chronic anxiety is low:
- Both individuality and togetherness can coexist
- People can disagree without threatening relationship bonds
- The group can tolerate uncertainty and complexity
- Thinking remains orderly and comprehensive

## Clinical Applications

### Assessment

1. **Distinguish acute from chronic anxiety** in the presenting problem
   - Acute: "What happened?" → identify the stressor and support adaptation
   - Chronic: "What is the pattern?" → identify the relationship process maintaining it
2. **Map the anxiety transmission pathway** through the family
   - Who absorbs anxiety? Who transmits it? Through what [triangles](triangles.md)?
3. **Assess the relationship between symptom onset and relationship disturbance**
   - Did symptoms emerge during or after a change in the family's emotional equilibrium?
   - What was the state of key relationships at the time of symptom onset?
4. **Evaluate the multigenerational anxiety pattern**
   - Is this person in a descending line of increasing undifferentiation and anxiety?
   - What is the level of chronic anxiety in the family of origin?
5. **Assess the role of the symptom in maintaining system equilibrium**
   - Does the symptom serve a stabilizing function for other relationships?
   - Would eliminating the symptom destabilize the system?

### Therapeutic Implications

1. **The therapist's primary task is managing their own anxiety** — an anxious therapist reinforces the family's anxiety rather than providing a non-anxious presence
2. **Avoid the tunnel vision trap** — do not elevate a facet of the problem to the cause of the problem. "An anxious family elevates a facet of a problem to the cause of the problem; if a therapist falls into the same trap, his potential value to the family is lost."
3. **Focus on the relationship process**, not on "why" individuals behave as they do. Asking "why" shifts the locus of control from the relationship to one person and immediately loses the systems perspective.
4. **Distinguish content from process** — the specific issues that generate conflict are less important than the pattern of reactivity surrounding them
5. **Support differentiation** — help people develop the capacity to think clearly in the presence of anxiety, rather than attempting to eliminate anxiety directly
6. **Identify the self-sustaining cycle** — help the family see how their reactions to the disturbance have become the primary generators of anxiety, independent of the original triggering event

### Key Therapeutic Principle

> When people can maintain comfortable contact with emotionally significant others, they are more likely to adapt successfully to events that are potentially stressful.

The goal of therapy is not to eliminate anxiety but to help people **maintain emotional contact while managing their own reactivity** — which is the definition of [differentiation](differentiation.md).

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---------------|------------|
| Chronic anxiety is caused by specific events | Events trigger acute anxiety; chronic anxiety is sustained by relationship process |
| The most anxious person is the sickest | The symptom bearer may be absorbing anxiety generated by the entire system |
| Reducing stress eliminates chronic anxiety | Chronic anxiety is relationship-based, not event-based; it persists even when external stressors are removed |
| Anxiety is purely psychological | Anxiety has manifestations from intracellular to societal levels; it is a biological process present in all living things |
| Children can be protected from parental anxiety | Trying to protect children is one of the main transmission mechanisms |
| Asking "why" someone is anxious helps | "Why" questions shift focus from the relationship system to the individual, losing the systems perspective |
| Chronic anxiety has a single cause | It is a system of actions and reactions that provides its own momentum, independent of any single trigger |
| High anxiety = low differentiation | They are correlated but distinct; well-differentiated people can experience high anxiety under sufficient stress |
