# Multigenerational Transmission Process

> Source: Kerr & Bowen, *Family Evaluation* (1988), Chapters 3–6; supplemented by Gilbert (2006)

## Overview

The multigenerational transmission process describes how the [nuclear family emotional process](nuclear-family-emotional-system.md), repeated across multiple generations, produces predictable outcomes in differentiation levels. Each generation's [family projection process](family-projection-process.md) selects certain children to receive more of the family's undifferentiation, and those children emerge less differentiated than their parents.

Over several generations, this creates **descending lines** of decreasing differentiation and **ascending lines** of increasing differentiation within the same extended family. Bowen considered this one of the most important concepts in family theory. It answers the fundamental question: *"When the nuclear family process proceeds over multiple generations, what is the outcome?"*

---

## Core Mechanism: Unequal Emotional Separation

### The Fundamental Process

The multigenerational transmission process is driven by four interlocking facts:

1. **Children from the same nuclear family have different degrees of emotional separation from their parents.** Not all siblings grow up with equal differentiation — the child most caught up in the family emotional process separates least; the child least involved separates most.

2. **People marry spouses with equivalent degrees of emotional separation from their families.** This is not a conscious choice but an emotionally driven selection process — people are attracted to others at similar levels of differentiation.

3. **Children of these new marriages have unequal degrees of emotional separation** and, in turn, marry people like themselves.

4. **This process repeats generation after generation**, eventually creating segments of family in which people have little emotional separation from one another, segments in which people are reasonably differentiated, and segments that reflect gradations between these extremes.

### Why Children Differ

The [Family Projection Process](family-projection-process.md) is the mechanism that creates differential outcomes among siblings. Not all children receive equal amounts of the family's emotional projection:

- **The most projected-to child** absorbs the most family anxiety, separates the least from parents, becomes the most relationship-dependent of the siblings, and emerges with the lowest level of differentiation
- **The least projected-to child** separates the most, is the least relationship-dependent, and emerges with the highest level of differentiation
- **Other siblings** fall at various points between these extremes

This selection is not random, nor is it something parents consciously "do to" a child. It is a **complementary process** in which both parents and child participate automatically. Bowen explicitly rejected the term "scapegoating" because it implies deliberate victimization rather than a natural systems process.

### Patterns in Which Child Is Selected

Research and clinical observation suggest the following patterns:

- **Oldest child** — a majority in schizophrenia research were oldest children
- **Oldest boy or oldest girl** — first child of a given gender often carries additional projection
- **Youngest child** — may be held closest, longest
- **Child born with a defect** — physical abnormality or illness makes a child a "sitting duck" for the projection process
- **Child born during a period of high parental anxiety** — the timing of birth relative to family stress matters (death of a grandparent, job loss, marital crisis)
- **Child who most resembles an emotionally significant figure** from the parents' own families

The selection operates through the [triangle](triangles.md) mechanism — the "focused child" triangle determines who receives the projection. The child occupies a specific position in the family's central triangle with the parents.

## The Descending Line

### How Differentiation Decreases Across Generations

Across generations, the most projected-to child in each generation:

1. Emerges with **lower differentiation** than the parents
2. Marries someone at a **similar (lower) differentiation level**
3. Produces a nuclear family with the **same or greater amount of undifferentiation**
4. The four nuclear family patterns (conflict, distance, overfunctioning/underfunctioning, triangulation) operate with **greater intensity**
5. Projects maximally to one of their own children
6. That child emerges with **still lower differentiation**

### The Gradient of Change

The change in differentiation from one generation to the next is usually **small** — perhaps a few points on Bowen's theoretical scale. But compounded over many generations, the effect is profound.

Over **8–10 generations**, a descending line can produce individuals with very low differentiation — vulnerable to serious dysfunction (schizophrenia, chronic physical illness, addiction, social failure) under relatively modest stress. The most emotionally involved child in a very poorly differentiated nuclear family (the outcome of many generations of gradual change) will absorb and generate **large amounts of chronic anxiety.**

### What Increases Along the Descending Line

Each generation in a descending line shows, on average:

- **Higher chronic anxiety** — more fear of what might be, more relationship dependence, more rumination
- **Greater emotional fusion** — less capacity to distinguish thinking from feeling, more reactivity
- **More relationship dependence** — functioning is more governed by the state of key relationships
- **More rigid nuclear family patterns** — the four patterns operate with greater intensity and less flexibility
- **Greater vulnerability to symptoms** — less stress is required to trigger physical, emotional, or social dysfunction
- **More active triangling** — the system's stability depends more heavily on triangling processes

### Clinical Implications of the Descending Line

Understanding the descending line is clinically important because it:

- **Explains severity without blame.** An individual with serious dysfunction (e.g., schizophrenia) is not "more defective" than others in the family — they are the endpoint of a multigenerational process. This reframe enables work on differentiation rather than blame.
- **Predicts vulnerability.** Knowing where a person falls in the multigenerational process helps predict what level of stress will trigger symptoms and what types of symptoms are most likely.
- **Contextualizes parenting.** Parents who understand the projection process can work on it. Bowen stated this is "something parents can do something about," making it one of the most practically important concepts.

## The Ascending Line

### How Differentiation Increases Across Generations

Simultaneously with the descending line, siblings who received **less projection** in each generation:

1. Emerge **more differentiated** than their parents
2. Marry at their (higher) level
3. Project **less** to their children
4. Their children emerge with still higher differentiation

Over many generations, this creates lines of **increasing differentiation, decreasing chronic anxiety, and greater adaptive capacity.** The least involved child in a well-differentiated family (the outcome of many generations of gradual upward change) will absorb and generate a **low level of chronic anxiety.**

### The Broad Family Picture

Within any extended family, the multigenerational process creates a **spread of differentiation levels** across branches:

- Some branches are at the low end — high fusion, high anxiety, high symptom vulnerability
- Some branches are at the high end — good differentiation, low chronic anxiety, high adaptive capacity
- Most branches are somewhere in between

This spread is observable on a well-constructed family diagram and provides the clinician with a map of the family's emotional terrain.

## The Role of Chronic Anxiety

The multigenerational transmission process is intimately linked to [chronic anxiety](chronic-anxiety.md):

- **Chronic anxiety levels show gradual increase** through the generations in descending lines
- **Chronic anxiety levels show gradual decrease** through the generations in ascending lines
- The average level of chronic anxiety of a person parallels the basic level of differentiation — **the lower the differentiation, the higher the average chronic anxiety**
- This component of chronic anxiety has **little to do with current life situation** — it is learned during the developmental years and carried through life
- The learning occurs on several levels:
  - The seemingly **osmotic absorption** of parental anxieties
  - The incorporation of **subjectively determined attitudes** that create anxiety (e.g., low self-esteem)
  - Physiological conditioning through prolonged association with anxious others

**Trying to protect children from one's own problems is actually one of the main ways problems are transmitted** from one generation to the next. The transmission process is too "wired in" to the way parents function, and children are highly prone to picking up anxiety.

## The Family Diagram (Genogram)

### Primary Tool for Tracking Multigenerational Process

The family diagram is the primary tool for observing and tracking the multigenerational transmission process. It provides a visual map of the family's emotional system across multiple generations.

### What the Family Diagram Maps

| Element | What It Reveals |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Family structure** | Births, deaths, marriages, divorces, separations across 3+ generations |
| **Relationship patterns** | Conflict, distance, cutoff, fusion between specific pairs |
| **Symptom concentration** | Which members developed physical, emotional, or social dysfunction — and when |
| **Sibling position** | Birth order, gender configuration, spacing — correlated with functional patterns |
| **Nodal events** | Deaths, births, marriages, divorces, migrations, job changes — and their timing relative to symptom onset |
| **Triangles** | Central and interlocking triangles across generations |
| **Projection pathways** | Which child in each generation absorbed the most family anxiety |
| **Cutoff patterns** | Which relationships were severed and when |
| **The flow of anxiety** | How chronic anxiety intensified or diminished across generational lines |

### Conventions

- **Squares** = males; **Circles** = females
- **Horizontal lines** = marriages/partnerships
- **Vertical lines** = parent-child relationships
- **Slash marks** = divorce; **X through symbol** = deceased
- **Relationship notation** varies by practitioner but typically includes:
  - Double lines = very close/fused
  - Zigzag lines = conflictual
  - Dashed lines = distant
  - Lines with break = cutoff

### How to Use the Family Diagram Clinically

1. **Construct the diagram collaboratively** with the client/family — the process of constructing it is itself therapeutic, as it shifts attention from the presenting problem to the broader family process
2. **Go back at least three generations** — the multigenerational pattern often only becomes visible with sufficient generational depth
3. **Note the timing of events** — symptom onset, deaths, births, marriages, and other nodal events often cluster in ways that reveal the anxiety transmission pathway
4. **Identify the projection pathway** — trace which child in each generation was most projected-to and how that person's life unfolded
5. **Look for parallel patterns** — the same triangle configurations, the same types of symptoms, the same sibling positions often repeat across generations
6. **Track differentiation lines** — identify ascending and descending lines within the extended family
7. **Use the diagram as a thinking tool** — it is not a diagnostic instrument but a framework for observing and organizing observations about the family emotional process

### What the Diagram Reveals That Single-Generation Assessment Cannot

The family diagram reveals patterns that are **invisible when looking at any single generation in isolation:**

- A child's behavioral problems may mirror an unresolved conflict between grandparents
- A pattern of eldest daughters carrying the family's emotional burden may repeat for generations
- The timing of symptom onset may correlate with anniversary reactions to multigenerational losses
- The family's current crisis may be a predictable outcome of processes set in motion several generations ago

> "Children may act out a conflict that was never resolved between their great-grandparents." (Chapter 6, Triangles)

## The Evolutionary Context

### Differentiation as Evolutionary Development

Bowen theory places the multigenerational transmission process in an evolutionary context. Drawing on the work of E. O. Wilson and Paul MacLean, the theory proposes that:

- The capacity for [differentiation of self](differentiation.md) is an evolutionary development presumably **unique to human beings** (or at least far more developed in humans)
- This capacity gives humans the ability to **simultaneously be an individual and a team player** — unlike colonial invertebrates, where functioning as part of a group requires giving up individuality
- The prefrontal cortex provides the neurological substrate for this capacity — it enables foresight, planning for others, and insight into the feelings of others
- Differentiation of self may be one of the factors that helped **reverse the one-billion-year antisocial trend** in vertebrate social evolution

### What This Means for the Multigenerational Process

The multigenerational transmission process is the mechanism by which the human capacity for differentiation is **developed or eroded** across generations:

- In ascending lines, the capacity for differentiation is progressively developed — individuals can maintain more individuality while remaining emotionally connected
- In descending lines, the capacity erodes — individuals become more governed by the togetherness force, more reactive, less able to think independently under pressure
- The process is not deterministic — any individual in any generation who works on their own differentiation can shift the trajectory for their descendants

### The Well-Differentiated Family vs. the Poorly Differentiated Family

Both types of families can look similar under calm conditions. The difference emerges under stress:

| Dimension | Well-Differentiated Family | Poorly Differentiated Family |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Cohesion basis** | Primarily individuality — people recognize realistic dependence but are fairly autonomous | Primarily togetherness — people cling to each other for emotional support |
| **Under stress** | Cohesion maintained; members can cooperate and look out for each other | Cohesion erodes; regression to selfish, aggressive, avoidance behaviors |
| **Altruism** | Sustained even in difficult periods | Breaks down under pressure |
| **Symptoms** | Require considerable stress to trigger | Can emerge with relatively modest stress |
| **Triangling** | Minimal; system stability does not depend on it | Intense; system stability depends on it |

## Clinical Applications

### Understanding — Not Blame

The most important clinical application of the multigenerational transmission process is that it **frees people from blame:**

- No one "caused" the problem — the emotional process has been operating for generations
- The presenting patient is not more defective than others — they occupy a particular position in a multigenerational process
- Parents are not villains — they are participants in a process that predates them
- This reframe enables people to **work on their own differentiation** rather than blaming parents or feeling victimized

### Prediction

Bowen believed the process is predictable enough that knowing the differentiation levels and family patterns at marriage could **forecast the kinds of problems a family will encounter.** While clinical practice does not permit the precision Bowen envisioned, the multigenerational perspective reliably identifies:

- Which family members are most vulnerable to symptom development
- What types of stressors are most likely to trigger symptoms
- What patterns are likely to repeat in the next generation

### Prevention

Parents who understand the projection process can work on it. This is one of the most practically important implications:

- **The goal is not to "stop projecting"** — the process is too automatic for that
- **The goal is to increase differentiation** — which naturally reduces the intensity of projection
- A parent who works on their own differentiation — managing their own anxiety, maintaining emotional contact without fusion — transmits **less chronic anxiety** to their children
- This shifts the trajectory for descendants without requiring that anyone be "fixed"

### Therapy Across Generations

The multigenerational perspective shapes therapeutic work:

1. **Construct and study the family diagram** — understanding the multigenerational process is the foundation of Bowen therapy
2. **Work on differentiation in the family of origin** — Bowen considered this the most powerful leverage point for change. Developing a more person-to-person relationship with parents and siblings — without cutoff and without fusion — changes the individual's functioning across all relationships
3. **Focus on the multigenerational process, not just the presenting problem** — the presenting problem is the surface manifestation of a much deeper process
4. **Identify and understand nodal events** — the family's critical events and how they were managed reveal the trajectory of the multigenerational process

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---------------|------------|
| This is genetic determinism | The process operates through emotional systems, not genes; any person who works on differentiation can shift the trajectory |
| Parents "cause" their children's problems | The process is complementary — children actively participate; and parents are themselves products of the same multigenerational process |
| The projected-to child is a scapegoat | Bowen rejected this term — it implies deliberate victimization rather than a natural systems process |
| All siblings have the same differentiation level | Unequal emotional separation is the engine of the entire process — siblings routinely differ significantly |
| The process is visible in one generation | The pattern only becomes clear across 3+ generations; single-generation analysis is inherently incomplete |
| Understanding the process is enough to change it | Understanding is necessary but not sufficient — change requires sustained work on differentiation, which means changing one's own functioning in key relationships |
